01 · Context

No auditing. No documentation.
No trail.

When I stepped into the GRC Analyst role, the SOC had no formal quality oversight. Cases were being closed with no mechanism to verify that analysts had followed required procedures. Leadership had no visibility into quality trends, and there was no process to surface, track, or remediate gaps when found.

The risk was operational and reputational: if any case had been selected for a customer audit or compliance review, there would have been no documented evidence of quality oversight — because none existed.

Before
  • No audit process, rubric, or scoring methodology
  • Findings surfaced verbally — nothing documented
  • No mechanism to track whether supervisors followed up
  • No risk register — risk was implied, never articulated
  • No KPIs, no trend data, no executive reporting
  • No way to close the loop between a finding and its remediation
After
  • Monthly audit cycle with defined scope, sample, and versioned rubric
  • Numbered findings registry tracking every issue to formal closure
  • Written documentation required for closure — verbal confirmation not accepted
  • Risk register with inherent vs. residual ratings and treatment decisions
  • Monthly executive dashboard with posture indicator and trend data
  • Full audit trail defensible to internal audit or customer review

04 · What I built

Five governance artifacts

Each artifact was designed to produce evidence, not just information. A governance program that cannot demonstrate its own outputs cannot defend itself to an auditor, a regulator, or a customer.

01
QA Scoring Rubric
Weighted criteria across documentation, severity classification, escalation handling, and case closure. Formally signed off by SOC leadership — making it an auditable document, not just a spreadsheet.
02
Findings Registry
Sequentially numbered log of every finding across all audit cycles. Includes criterion failed, root cause category, assigned owner, status, and days open. IDs never restart — maintaining historical traceability for the life of the program.
03
Risk Register
Formal risk statements with inherent and residual risk ratings and documented treatment decisions for every Major and Critical finding. Converts audit findings into governance-grade risk language for leadership and auditors.
04
Executive Dashboard & Report
Monthly deliverable combining a posture indicator, supervisor team performance trends, score distribution, root cause analysis, and management response tracking — structured so leadership can read the summary alone and fully understand program health.
05
Coaching Documentation Framework
Formalized requirement that supervisor follow-ups on findings be documented and submitted to QA. Defines what constitutes acceptable evidence vs. what does not. Creates accountability that does not depend on memory or goodwill.

06 · GRC Report Design

Reporting that earns decisions

The monthly GRC report I produce for leadership is not a status update — it is a governance document. The distinction is structural: every section answers a specific question, evidence requirements are defined before the cycle starts, and the executive summary is written so that leadership can read it alone and fully understand program health without looking at a single chart.

The design rationale below explains the choices behind the structure. These are not defaults — each is a deliberate decision that makes the report more defensible and more useful to its audience.

Why It Matters

Why the report is structured this way — key design decisions:

Bottom line before everything
Leadership should never have to read to the end of a report to understand the headline. The bottom line is one sentence and appears first — not buried in findings. If leadership reads nothing else, they know the most important thing.
Posture criteria defined in writing
Green, Yellow, and Red are defined by specific, documented thresholds that do not change month to month without a methodology update. A rating that means different things each cycle is not a rating — it is a mood. Stable criteria let leadership compare posture across time.
CISA four-part finding structure
Every Major and Critical finding is documented using Condition / Criteria / Cause / Effect. This structure forces precision: Condition is the observable fact, Criteria is the rule violated, Cause is the root reason (not the symptom), Effect is what could go wrong. Without this structure, findings are opinions. With it, they are evidence.
Management response documented or flagged
Every recommendation has a corresponding management response field. If no response was received, the field reads "Management response pending as of [date]" — not blank. A blank field is ambiguous. Pending is a visible, trackable status that creates accountability without being adversarial.
Root cause as a program-level metric
Findings are classified by root cause category every cycle, which means the report can surface systemic patterns: Training Gaps that recur three months in a row despite individual coaching indicate a program failure, not an analyst failure. The fix is different depending on which it is.
07 · Risk Assessment Methodology

From threat to treatment decision

The following is a documented qualitative risk assessment conducted against the analyst platform access control environment — the SIEM, ticketing system, and automation platform used by SOC analysts daily. The methodology is aligned to NIST CSF 2.0's ID.RA (Risk Assessment) subcategory, with risk rating using a qualitative likelihood × impact scale and risk statements structured to support formal treatment decisions.

This assessment is anonymized and illustrative of the methodology applied in practice. Each risk is documented using the same four-part structure used in QA findings — ensuring that risk language is consistent across audit, GRC, and risk management functions.

Methodology note: This assessment uses qualitative scoring (Rare / Unlikely / Possible / Likely / Almost Certain for likelihood; Negligible / Minor / Moderate / Major / Severe for impact). FAIR quantitative concepts — specifically the distinction between Threat Event Frequency and Vulnerability — inform the likelihood analysis but full Monte Carlo simulation is outside the scope of this assessment. Inherent risk is rated without controls; residual risk is rated with current controls applied.

Three things this walkthrough is designed to demonstrate: first, that risk rating methodology is explicit — likelihood and impact are stated separately before a composite rating is assigned, which prevents the composite from being arbitrary. Second, that existing controls are named precisely — not "we have access controls" but which control, doing what, with what limitation. Third, that treatment decisions include the reasoning — Accept is a valid treatment option, but it requires documentation of what monitoring is in place and what would trigger re-evaluation.

08 · AI Risk Assessment & Scoring Controls

Assessing the AI scoring layer

The organization is currently implementing Torq, an automation platform that uses AI to automatically score incident cases against quality criteria. Before any deployment decisions were made, I conducted a formal risk assessment of the AI scoring layer — identifying what could go wrong, designing compensating controls, and documenting the methodology so the AI layer is as auditable as the manual layer it augments.

This is distinct from governing enterprise AI policy. The scope is specific: one platform, one use case, documented risk treatment for each identified risk category, tracked in the program risk register.

1
AI Auto-Scoring — all cases
Torq automatically scores every closed incident case against defined quality criteria. Scores are logged, time-stamped, and retained as part of the audit trail. This creates continuous coverage that manual sampling alone cannot achieve.
100% of cases
2
Below-threshold mandatory review — flagged cases
Any case scoring below 70% is automatically flagged and routed for mandatory manual QA review. The AI score is not accepted as a final determination — it is treated as a trigger for human analysis. This prevents AI scoring errors from producing unchallenged findings against analysts.
Scores < 70%
3
10% spot-check validation — compensating control
A random 10% of all monthly cases are manually reviewed regardless of AI score. This validates AI scoring accuracy, generates an alignment rate metric, and surfaces cases where the AI scored above 70% but a human reviewer would have scored below. This is the primary control against AI model drift and systematic scoring bias.
10% random sample

AI risks formally assessed during scoping: scoring inaccuracy due to case type distribution shifts; over-reliance on AI without human validation layer; audit trail gaps if AI decision logic is not documented; and scoring bias across case types that may systematically disadvantage specific analysts or teams. Each risk is tracked in the risk register with assigned treatment decisions.

10 · Design Decisions

The choices that weren't obvious

The structure of this program did not come from a template. These are the specific decisions I made and the reasoning behind each one.

Sequential Finding IDs that never restart
Finding F-2026-047 is the forty-seventh finding of 2026 — period. If IDs restart, historical references break and the audit trail becomes unverifiable across reporting cycles. An auditor must be able to trace any ID back to its source with no ambiguity.
Written documentation required — verbal confirmation not accepted
If a supervisor says a coaching session happened but submits nothing, the field is blank. Not a gap in the system — a factual absence of evidence. A QA program that accepts verbal assurances cannot attest to its own oversight quality. This rule creates accountability without being adversarial.
Inherent and residual risk as distinct ratings
Rating both answers the question leadership actually needs: how much is this program reducing risk, not just documenting it? If both ratings are High, the controls are not working. This framing makes the program's value measurable rather than implied.
Root cause categories assigned to every finding
Classifying findings by root cause — Training Gap, Process Gap, Tooling Issue, Workload/Volume, Rubric Gap — means leadership can see whether intervention should be individual coaching, an SOP update, or a Torq configuration change. The same finding count means completely different things depending on root cause.
AI score as trigger — not as final determination
A Torq score below 70% routes the case to mandatory manual review. The AI score opens an investigation; it does not close one. This prevents AI scoring errors from producing unchallenged adverse findings against analysts and maintains human accountability in every quality determination.
Posture rating with documented, stable criteria
Green, Yellow, and Red are defined by specific, written thresholds applied consistently month over month. A rating that means different things each cycle is not a rating leadership can rely on for decisions. The criteria are published and do not change without a documented methodology update.

12 · What I'd improve

Where the program goes next

A governance program that isn't improving is drifting toward obsolescence. These are the specific improvements I'm actively working toward or would prioritize with additional resourcing.

Tooling
Migrate the findings registry into Torq. The current Excel-based tracker works, but it requires manual data entry and does not surface real-time status. A platform-native registry would allow findings to be visible and updated between monthly report cycles without a separate manual process.
Process
Formalize target date logic by severity. Right now, target dates are set judgment by judgment. A documented standard — Minor findings close by end of month, Major within two weeks of the 1:1, Critical within 10–14 days of report issuance — reduces inconsistency and makes SLA adherence trackable.
Program
Connect SOC findings to the enterprise risk register. The program-level risk register I built captures QA and AI governance risks within the SOC. The maturity step is contributing high-severity or recurring findings upward into an enterprise view, where they can inform broader organizational risk posture.
Tooling
Add structured linkage between MIRT cases and open findings. Currently the connection between major incident review cases and QA findings is written narratively in the monthly report. A tagged, queryable linkage would let leadership see exactly which incidents trace back to documented quality gaps — making the operational case for QA investment quantifiable.
Process
Build a documented quarterly rubric review cadence. The rubric is currently revised reactively when a Rubric Gap finding surfaces. A scheduled quarterly review — with version control and published change notes — would make rubric evolution a governance event rather than an incident response.
